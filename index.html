Research

Primary

Image-plane transients

Radio variability occurs on a wide range of timescales, from nanoseconds to years. Image-plane transients, or "slow" transients, vary on timescales of seconds and longer, and in recent years "long-period transients" (LPTs) have emerged as a new class, with periods ranging from minutes to hours and pulse durations of seconds to minutes. To date, only a handful of LPTs have been discovered, and all within the Galaxy. Identifying the emission mechanism/s for LPTs is still challenging, motivating efforts to increase the known population of these objects. For my PhD, I am conducting a search of the Magellanic Clouds for LPTs and image-plane transients in general, to determine whether such populations exist beyond our Galaxy. We are using archival data from MeerKAT, which is the most sensitive radio telescope in the Southern Hemisphere, to search over timescales from several seconds up to several years, using a combination of the Polkat and TRON (Transient Radio Observations for Newbies) pipelines.

Secondary/Previous

Fast Radio Bursts

I'm also interested in Fast Radio Bursts (FRBs), particularly whether or not they have multi-wavelength counterparts. For my MSc research, I was involved in a project that aimed to identify potential optical counterparts to FRBs using data from the MeerLICHT telescope in South Africa. MeerLICHT is 0.6-m optical telescope that was specifically commissioned to operate simultaneously with MeerKAT. We thus had the opportunity to search for optical counterparts to not only repeating FRBs, but non-repeating ones too.
